Operational Continuity for Community Events: A Practical Living Ops Manual

Community events often succeed because a small group carries the plan in their heads. It looks smooth from the outside, but inside the team it usually means late nights, repeated confusion, and the same volunteers absorbing most of the pressure because they are the only ones who know what happens next.

This talk shares a practical way to build a living ops manual for community-run events. It is not a long document that no one reads. It is a set of runbooks, templates, checklists, and decision notes that helps teams share work, onboard new volunteers quickly, and reduce stress during crunch time. We will walk through a simple structure that can be copied, how to capture decisions as reusable assets, and how to keep it updated without adding extra burden to the team.

Outline

  1. 1. The problem we all recognize (3 min)
    • When event operations live in people’s heads
    • Common symptoms: repeated questions, decision bottlenecks, last-minute scrambling, volunteer fatigue
  2. 2. What a “living ops manual” means in practice (4 min)
    • Not a long narrative document
    • A working set of artifacts the team actually uses: runbooks, checklists, templates, decision notes, owners
  3. 3. The minimum viable structure (7 min)
    • Organizing by timeline: Before / During / After
    • Organizing by function: program, sponsorship, venue, communications, volunteers, finance, Code of Conduct
    • Where each artifact lives, how it’s named, and how people find the right thing fast
  4. 4. Turning decisions into reusable assets (7 min)
    • Converting recurring conversations into templates and SOPs
    • Examples to walk through (community-event focused):
    • Volunteer onboarding pack (roles, expectations, escalation)
    • Speaker communication templates (acceptance, prep, day-of)
    • Day-of operations checklist (registration, AV, signage, backups)
    • Simple escalation path (who decides what, when)
  5. 5. Keeping it alive without adding work (5 min)
    • “Micro-updates” after decisions: quick notes that prevent future confusion
    • Retrospectives that produce artifacts: what to capture, how to assign owners
    • Versioning and handoff: what new leads need, what to archive
  6. 6. How to start next week (4 min)
    • The first five pages to create
    • The fastest wins that reduce load immediately
    • A simple 30-day rhythm: build, use, refine
